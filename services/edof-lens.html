Understanding EDOF Technology

What Is an EDOF Lens?

An Extended Depth of Focus (EDOF) intraocular lens takes a fundamentally different approach to vision correction than a traditional multifocal IOL. Rather than splitting light into separate focal points using concentric diffractive rings, an EDOF lens elongates a single focal zone into a continuous range. This creates a smooth, uninterrupted corridor of clear vision stretching from distance through intermediate — the range where most of daily life happens. The result is high-contrast, natural-feeling vision with significantly fewer halos and glare than multifocal alternatives.

Several EDOF platforms are available today, each using distinct optical engineering. The TECNIS Symfony employs echelette diffraction to elongate focal depth while correcting chromatic aberration. The AcrySof IQ Vivity uses X-WAVE technology — a non-diffractive wavefront-shaping surface — to stretch the range of vision without the ring patterns that cause nighttime halos. At Soni Vision Institute, our fellowship-trained surgeons are experienced with all major EDOF platforms and will recommend the lens that best matches your anatomy, visual demands, and lifestyle goals.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How is an EDOF lens different from a multifocal lens?

A multifocal lens uses concentric diffractive rings to split light into distinct focal points for near, intermediate, and distance vision. An EDOF lens takes a fundamentally different approach — it elongates a single focal point into a continuous range, creating a seamless corridor of vision from distance through intermediate. Because EDOF lenses do not split light into separate zones, they produce significantly fewer halos and glare than multifocal designs, making them an excellent choice for patients who prioritize visual quality and contrast sensitivity.

Will I still need reading glasses with an EDOF lens?

EDOF lenses deliver excellent distance and intermediate vision — activities like driving, computer work, and cooking are typically glasses-free. For fine print or prolonged close-up reading in low light, some patients may still prefer a pair of low-power reading glasses. However, many patients find they can manage most near tasks comfortably, especially in good lighting. Your surgeon can discuss strategies such as blended vision to maximize your reading range.

Who is the best candidate for an EDOF lens?

EDOF lenses are ideal for active patients who want excellent distance and intermediate vision with minimal optical side effects. They are particularly well-suited for patients who drive frequently at night, work on computers, and value high contrast vision. Patients who are not good candidates for multifocal lenses — due to mild corneal irregularities, prior refractive surgery, or sensitivity to halos — often do very well with EDOF technology. Your surgeon will evaluate your eye health, visual demands, and expectations to determine the best lens for you.
